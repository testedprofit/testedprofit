Viola Liuzzo Memorial

A solemn marker on U.S. Highway 80 honoring the only white woman killed in the civil rights movement struggle in the South.

The Ultimate Sacrifice

Viola Liuzzo, a 39-year-old mother from Detroit, traveled to Alabama after witnessing "Bloody Sunday."

On March 25, 1965, while shuttling marchers back to Selma, she was pursued and killed by Klansmen. Her death helped galvanize support for the Voting Rights Act of 1965.

Sacred Ground

Located on U.S. Highway 80, 20 miles east of Selma on the Selma to Montgomery National Historic Trail.

The granite monument, erected in 1991, reads: "In memory of our sister Viola Liuzzo who gave her life in the struggle for the right to vote."

A Catalyst for Justice

Despite an initial smear campaign by the FBI to cover up the involvement of an informant, history has reclaimed Viola Liuzzo's legacy. Her murder added critical impetus to President Johnson's efforts, leading to the passage of the Voting Rights Act just five months later.

"A powerful reminder of the sacrifices made by individuals from all walks of life who supported the struggle for freedom."
