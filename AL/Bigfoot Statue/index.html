Bigfoot Capital of Alabama

A 4-foot-tall sculpture in Evergreen, Alabama, celebrating the town's official designation and legendary sightings.

Official Designation

Unanimously named the "Bigfoot Capital of Alabama" by the City Council in February 2017.

The designation followed numerous reported sightings of the legendary creature within a 20-mile radius of the city.

History & Background

Alabama's Bigfoot Statue taps into one of the most persistent and beloved pieces of American folklore — the legend of a large, bipedal, hair-covered creature living in the forests of North America. While Bigfoot is most associated with the Pacific Northwest, reports of similar creatures under various names (Skunk Ape in Florida, Swamp Ape in the Southeast) come from across the United States, including Alabama's dense forests and swampy bottomlands. The Alabama-Coushatta tribe and other Indigenous peoples of the Southeast have oral traditions describing similar beings, suggesting the legend has deep roots in the region's cultural history.

Alabama has genuine Bigfoot folklore centered in its heavily forested regions — particularly the Bankhead National Forest in the northwest and the extensive longleaf pine and hardwood forests of the southern part of the state. These forests, with their abundance of wildlife and limited human access, provide exactly the kind of habitat that Bigfoot enthusiasts associate with the creature. The Bigfoot Field Researchers Organization has documented dozens of alleged sightings in Alabama over the decades.

A Bigfoot statue in Alabama serves the same function as similar statues across the country: it gives travelers a reason to stop, invites engagement with local folklore and humor, and creates a memorable landmark in communities that might otherwise be overlooked. Roadside Bigfoot statues also speak to something genuine in American culture — a persistent delight in mystery, a love of the wild and unknown, and a sense that the forests and swamps and mountains of this vast country might still contain things we haven't fully catalogued. Alabama's forests are deep enough and old enough to sustain that sense of wonder.

Nearby Attractions

Bankhead National Forest

Alabama's largest national forest, covering 181,000 acres of the Cumberland Plateau in northwest Alabama. Bankhead features Sipsey Wilderness — the first designated wilderness area east of the Mississippi River — with dramatic sandstone canyons, waterfalls, and old-growth forest. Hiking trails, swimming holes, and wildlife viewing opportunities make it one of Alabama's premier outdoor destinations.

Conecuh National Forest

In south Alabama, the Conecuh National Forest protects longleaf pine habitat that once covered the entire coastal plain of the Southeast. The 21-mile Conecuh Trail winds through pitcher plant bogs, springs, and restored longleaf forest. The forest is managed partly for the red-cockaded woodpecker, an endangered species dependent on old-growth longleaf pine, providing excellent wildlife observation opportunities.

Talladega National Forest

Central Alabama's Talladega National Forest contains Cheaha Mountain — at 2,413 feet, the highest point in Alabama — and Cheaha State Park. The Pinhoti Trail, a long-distance hiking trail connecting to the Appalachian Trail system in Georgia, passes through the forest. Rock formations, waterfalls, and panoramic views of the Alabama Piedmont reward hikers who explore the area.
