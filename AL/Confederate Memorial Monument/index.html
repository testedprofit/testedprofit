Confederate Memorial Monument

An 88-foot obelisk standing on the north lawn of the Alabama State Capitol, dedicated to the 122,000 Alabamians who served in the Civil War.

Stone & Bronze

Designed by Alexander Doyle and dedicated in 1898. Features four granite statues representing military branches and a bronze figure of "Patriotism" at the top.

In the Shadow of History

The monument sits in a complex historical landscape, situated on the Capitol grounds near the Dexter Avenue King Memorial Baptist Church and the Civil Rights Memorial. It stands as a physical representation of the Deep South's conflicting narratives—honoring Confederate heritage while existing within a city pivotal to the Civil Rights Movement.

History & Background

Confederate memorial monuments were erected throughout the American South in large numbers during two distinct periods: the 1890s-1910s (coinciding with the height of Jim Crow laws and disfranchisement of Black voters) and the 1950s-1960s (coinciding with the Civil Rights Movement). Alabama, as a Confederate state and later a central battleground of the Civil Rights Movement, has numerous Confederate monuments that have become focal points in the ongoing national debate about public commemoration and historical memory.

The Civil War (1861–1865) was the defining catastrophe of American history, killing approximately 620,000 soldiers and transforming the society and politics of the nation. Alabama supplied approximately 90,000 soldiers to the Confederacy and suffered significant military operations throughout the state, particularly in the Tennessee Valley and Mobile Bay areas. The Confederacy's capital was initially Montgomery, Alabama, where Jefferson Davis was inaugurated as Confederate president in February 1861. The war ended slavery and forced a fundamental transformation of Southern society, though that transformation was systematically reversed during the Reconstruction and Jim Crow eras.

Confederate monuments in Alabama and throughout the South are now at the center of intense public debate about what communities choose to honor in public spaces. The Alabama Memorial Preservation Act (2017) makes removing or relocating Confederate monuments legally complicated, creating ongoing controversy. Many communities are grappling with how to contextualize these monuments — whether through added interpretive markers, relocation to museum settings, or other approaches — as part of a broader American reckoning with the legacy of slavery and racial inequality. Visiting a Confederate monument provides an opportunity to engage with this complex history and the ongoing debates it generates.

Nearby Attractions

National Memorial for Peace and Justice (Montgomery)

The nation's first memorial dedicated to the victims of racial terror lynching provides essential historical context for understanding the relationship between Confederate commemoration, the Lost Cause mythology, and the racial violence of the post-Civil War era. Created by Bryan Stevenson's Equal Justice Initiative, it is among the most important memorials built in the United States in recent decades.

First White House of the Confederacy (Montgomery)

The house where Confederate President Jefferson Davis and his family lived during the early months of the Confederacy is preserved as a museum. It provides direct historical context for understanding the Confederate government that Alabama hosted and the society the Confederacy sought to preserve. Interpretive materials engage seriously with the history of slavery and secession.

Alabama Department of Archives and History (Montgomery)

One of the oldest state archives in the United States, Alabama's archives building adjacent to the state capitol houses extensive historical collections including Civil War records, plantation records, Reconstruction-era documents, and Civil Rights era materials. The museum portion provides a comprehensive narrative of Alabama history that contextualizes Confederate monuments within the full scope of the state's past.
