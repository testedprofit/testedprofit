Nathan Bedford Forrest Monument

A controversial memorial located in the Confederate Memorial Circle within the Old Live Oak Cemetery in Selma, Alabama.

A Divisive Figure

Honors a Confederate General who was also a slave trader and the first Grand Wizard of the KKK.

Recognized by supporters as the "Defender of Selma" during the Civil War, but viewed by critics as a symbol of hate and oppression.

History of Controversy

The monument's presence in Selma, a city synonymous with the Civil Rights Movement, highlights conflicting historical narratives in the South. Its journey from a city park to the cemetery has been marked by protests, theft, and heated public debate.

History & Background

Nathan Bedford Forrest (1821–1877) was a Confederate general during the Civil War and the first Grand Wizard of the Ku Klux Klan. Born in Chapel Hill, Tennessee, Forrest became wealthy as a slave trader in Memphis before the Civil War — one of the South's most successful dealers in enslaved human beings. During the war, he commanded Confederate cavalry with genuine tactical brilliance, winning engagements through aggressive movement and surprise. He is considered by military historians to have been an innovative and effective commander who influenced subsequent cavalry tactics.

Forrest is also responsible for the Fort Pillow Massacre (April 12, 1864), in which Confederate forces under his command killed a disproportionate number of Black Union soldiers attempting to surrender at Fort Pillow, Tennessee. The massacre was widely condemned in the North as evidence of Confederate atrocities against Black troops. After the war, Forrest founded or became the first leader of the Ku Klux Klan in 1865-1867, an organization that used terror, murder, and intimidation to suppress Black political participation during Reconstruction. Though he later claimed to have disbanded the Klan and distanced himself from its violence, his founding role is historically unambiguous.

Monuments to Nathan Bedford Forrest are among the most controversial in the United States, having been removed from numerous locations across the South in recent years as communities have reconsidered what their public spaces should honor. Alabama, like many Southern states, has grappled with laws protecting Confederate monuments that make removal legally complicated. Any existing Forrest monument in Alabama exists within this highly contested space, where the question of what a community's public art communicates about its values is actively debated. Visiting such a monument provides an opportunity to engage directly with these ongoing conversations about history, memory, and public space in the American South.

Nearby Attractions

National Memorial for Peace and Justice (Montgomery)

The Equal Justice Initiative's memorial to victims of racial terror lynching provides essential historical counterpoint to Confederate monuments in Alabama. The names of thousands of Black Americans who were lynched — many of them in the same counties where Confederate monuments stand — are inscribed on hanging steel columns. This memorial, along with the Legacy Museum, offers a comprehensive and painful account of racial terror in the post-Civil War South.

Birmingham Civil Rights Institute

One of the finest civil rights museums in the United States, the BCRI documents the full history of the Civil Rights Movement with particular depth on Birmingham's role. Interactive exhibits, historical footage, and powerful artifacts trace the struggle from Reconstruction through the 1960s and into the present. Located across from Kelly Ingram Park, where fire hoses and police dogs were used against demonstrators in 1963, the setting gives the museum particular power.

Alabama Department of Archives and History

For those seeking to understand the full complexity of Alabama history — including the Reconstruction era, the rise of the Klan, disfranchisement, and Jim Crow — the Alabama Archives in Montgomery is the primary research resource. The museum portion provides a comprehensive narrative of Alabama history available free to the public. Understanding this history in its full context is essential for making sense of the Confederate monument landscape visitors encounter in Alabama today.
