Hank Williams Statue

A life-size bronze monument in downtown Montgomery, honoring the legendary singer-songwriter who defined an era of country music.

Life-Size Legend

Created by sculptors Doug and Sandra McDonald, taking over 1,800 hours to complete.

Stands exactly 6'2"—Hank's actual height—depicting him in a classic stage pose with his trademark cowboy hat and guitar.

Riverfront District

Located at 216 Commerce Street in the heart of the Riverfront Entertainment District.

Moved in 2016 for better visibility

Originally unveiled in 1991

More Than A Statue

The statue is just the beginning. One block away stands the Hank Williams Museum, housing his famous 1952 baby blue Cadillac. A short drive leads to the Oakwood Cemetery Annex, the final resting place of the music icon.

History & Background

Hank Williams (1923–1953) is the most important figure in the history of country music — a singer, songwriter, and performer from Georgiana, Alabama whose recordings fundamentally shaped the genre and influenced virtually every country artist who followed him. Williams grew up in poverty in southern Alabama, learning guitar as a boy from Rufus "Tee-Tot" Payne, an African American blues musician in Georgiana, and absorbing the sacred harp singing, gospel, and blues traditions of the rural Deep South. These influences gave his music an emotional directness and authenticity that remains unmatched.

Between 1947 and his death on January 1, 1953, Williams recorded some of the most enduring songs in American music: "Your Cheatin' Heart," "Hey, Good Lookin'," "I'm So Lonesome I Could Cry," "Jambalaya (On the Bayou)," "Cold Cold Heart," "Long Gone Lonesome Blues," and dozens more. He was a member of the Grand Ole Opry and became its biggest star, selling out venues across the country. Tony Bennett's recording of "Cold Cold Heart" brought Williams's songs to mainstream pop audiences and established a pattern of country-pop crossover that continues today. Williams died at age 29 in the back seat of his Cadillac, en route to a show, of heart failure attributed in part to alcohol and drug use.

The Hank Williams statue in Montgomery — where Williams is also buried — honors a man whose brief life produced an extraordinary body of work. Montgomery was Williams's adopted city, where he launched his professional career and achieved his greatest success. The statue, the museum, and his grave site together make Montgomery one of the most significant destinations in American music history. Every year, thousands of music pilgrims visit to pay respects to the man who defined country music's emotional core.

Nearby Attractions

Hank Williams Museum (Montgomery)

Located in downtown Montgomery, the Hank Williams Museum houses his 1952 Cadillac convertible (the car in which he died), stage costumes, handwritten song lyrics, guitars, and extensive memorabilia documenting his career. The museum provides comprehensive coverage of Williams's life and music in a building on the same street as many of his Montgomery landmarks. Essential for any country music fan visiting Alabama.

Hank Williams Sr. Gravesite

Williams is buried at Oakwood Cemetery Annex in Montgomery. His grave, marked by a marble monument with musical notes and a cowboy hat, is one of the most visited music pilgrimages in the South. Thousands of fans leave guitar picks, flowers, and notes at the grave. The adjacent grave of his wife, Audrey Williams, is also frequently visited. The cemetery is open daily during daylight hours.

W.C. Handy Home and Museum (Florence)

Florence, Alabama in the Shoals area is the birthplace of W.C. Handy, the "Father of the Blues" — another Alabama musician of foundational importance in American music. The Shoals region also produced Wilson Pickett, Percy Sledge, and the session musicians at Muscle Shoals Sound Studio who played on some of the most important recordings of the 1960s and 1970s. Alabama's musical heritage is extraordinary.
