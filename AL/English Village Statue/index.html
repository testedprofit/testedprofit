Digital Landmark Profile

Civitas: The Spirit of Community

A bronze sculpture by James Barnhill standing as the gateway to English Village. A tribute to community strength and architectural heritage.

"Civitas"

Latin for "Citizens" or "Community."

The name reflects the tight-knit nature and community spirit of the English Village area in Mountain Brook, Alabama.

Making a Place

Evokes the memory of Carolyn Smith, the self-taught architect who designed many English Village buildings in the 1920s.

The figure is not a literal depiction but captures the spirit of creating a place. Designed to project strength over conventional beauty.

Gateway to the Village

Dedicated in 1998, this "gateway" piece depicts a female figure examining models of the village buildings. It serves as a focal point at the entrance of the commercial district, welcoming visitors to the European-inspired boutiques and restaurants.

"A prominent and symbolic landmark that helps define the unique character and history of English Village."

History & Background

English Village is a distinctive shopping and cultural district in Mountain Brook, Alabama — one of the most affluent communities in the Birmingham metropolitan area. Developed in the 1920s and 1930s with Tudor Revival architecture inspired by English village design, the district was intended to evoke the charm of a traditional English market town. Mountain Brook itself was designed by landscape architect Warren Manning and developer Robert Jemison Jr. as a planned garden suburb, making it one of the most complete examples of early 20th-century American planned community design in the South.

The English Village sculpture reflects the district's identity as a curated, aesthetically unified commercial space that has maintained its architectural character for nearly a century. Unlike most American commercial strips that have been rebuilt multiple times, English Village retains its original buildings and scale — a relatively rare survival of 1920s commercial architecture in Alabama. The shops, restaurants, and public spaces of English Village serve as a gathering place for Mountain Brook's community and attract visitors from throughout the Birmingham area.

Mountain Brook's history is also intertwined with Birmingham's racial history. Like many wealthy white suburbs throughout the American South, Mountain Brook developed in part as a retreat from Birmingham's urban diversity, and its zoning and social history reflect the patterns of residential segregation that shaped metropolitan Birmingham throughout the 20th century. Visiting English Village offers an opportunity to engage with both the genuine architectural charm of the district and the larger historical context in which wealthy suburban enclaves were developed during the era of legal segregation.

Nearby Attractions

Birmingham Museum of Art

The largest municipal art museum in the Southeast, the Birmingham Museum of Art houses over 27,000 works spanning 5,000 years of art history. The collection includes exceptional Asian decorative arts, American art, European paintings and sculpture, and African art. Free admission makes it one of the best values in Birmingham. The museum's sculpture garden provides a lovely outdoor space adjacent to Linn Park.

Vulcan Park and Museum

Vulcan — the Roman god of fire and the forge — stands 56 feet tall atop Red Mountain overlooking Birmingham. At 101,000 pounds, it is the largest cast iron statue in the world, created for the 1904 St. Louis World's Fair to showcase Birmingham's iron industry. The observation platform provides panoramic views of the Birmingham metropolitan area, and the museum tells the story of the city's iron and steel heritage.

Red Mountain Park

Situated on the ridge that gave Birmingham its iron ore and steel industry, Red Mountain Park offers over 1,500 acres of outdoor recreation including extensive trail systems, zip lines, and historic iron ore mining sites interpreted for the public. The park sits directly above some of Birmingham's most historic neighborhoods and provides excellent views of the city while telling the story of the geology and industry that created it.
