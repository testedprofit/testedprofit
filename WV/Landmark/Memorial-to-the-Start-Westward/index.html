The Sculpture

Sculpted by Gutzon Borglum (famous for Mount Rushmore), this massive sandstone pylon features five limestone figures representing the nation's expansion westward.

The figures depict the spirit of the pioneer, looking out toward the Muskingum River, symbolizing the gateway to the new frontier.

"Here, at the confluence of the rivers, a new chapter of American history began, carving a path for the nation's future."

History & Background

The Memorial to the Start Westward commemorates the beginning of westward American expansion — a theme of profound significance in West Virginia because this Appalachian state sat at the very edge of the original frontier. The Proclamation Line of 1763, established by the British after the Seven Years' War, ran approximately through the Appalachian ridge and prohibited American colonists from settling west of the mountains. The lands that became West Virginia were at the vanguard of illegal settlement that eventually drove British-Native alliances and contributed to the causes of the American Revolution.

Daniel Boone, one of America's most famous frontier figures, explored and settled in the regions that are now West Virginia and Kentucky. The Kanawha Valley in West Virginia was a major pathway for settlers moving westward through the Cumberland Gap and along river routes into Kentucky and beyond. Early West Virginia was truly the jumping-off point — the last edge of the eastern colonial world before the vast, unknown interior of the continent. Settlers who passed through carried with them everything needed to build new lives from wilderness.

The westward expansion that began in the Appalachian foothills carried enormous consequences: the displacement of Indigenous peoples across the continent, the extension of slavery into new territories, and ultimately the violent resolution of the question of slavery in the Civil War. West Virginia's unique position in this history — as a state formed in opposition to slavery's expansion and Confederate secession — gives a westward expansion memorial here a layered significance. It honors the courage of pioneers while existing in a state whose very birth was shaped by the contradictions and conflicts that westward movement produced.

Nearby Attractions

Grave Creek Mound Archaeological Complex

Located in Moundsville, West Virginia, this enormous earthwork — 69 feet tall and 295 feet in diameter — was built by the Adena culture approximately 2,000 years ago. It is the largest conical burial mound in the United States. The adjacent Delf Norona Museum interprets the Adena and Hopewell cultures that built extensive mound complexes throughout the Ohio Valley region before European contact.

Blennerhassett Island Historical State Park

Accessible only by sternwheeler ferry from Parkersburg, this Ohio River island was the site of Harman and Margaret Blennerhassett's remarkable Federal-era mansion and estate — and the alleged staging point for Aaron Burr's mysterious 1806 conspiracy. The reconstructed mansion, costumed interpreters, and horse-drawn wagon tours create an immersive experience of early American frontier aristocracy.

Wheeling's Historic District

Wheeling was a vital gateway to the West through the 19th century, served by the National Road (America's first federally funded highway) and later by the Baltimore and Ohio Railroad. The downtown historic district contains fine Victorian commercial architecture, and the Wheeling Suspension Bridge (1849) was the longest wire suspension bridge in the world when built. The Capitol Music Hall and West Virginia Independence Hall (where West Virginia's statehood was proclaimed) are among the key sites.
