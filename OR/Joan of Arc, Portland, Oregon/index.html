The Golden Rider

Magnificent Design

Depicting the French heroine Jeanne d’Arc in armor, triumphantly hoisting a banner. It is a replica of an 1874 work by French sculptor Emmanuel Frémiet, with sister statues in Paris, Philadelphia, and New Orleans.

Laurelhurst Gateway

Memorial & Restoration

Donated in 1924 by Dr. Henry Waldo Coe to honor American soldiers. After being damaged in 2020, it underwent significant restoration and re-gilding with brilliant 23-karat gold leaf before returning in October 2022.

History & Background

Joan of Arc (c.1412–1431) was a French peasant girl from Domrémy who claimed to receive divine visions directing her to support Charles VII and recover France from English domination during the Hundred Years War. At age 17, she convinced the French royal court to allow her to lead troops. Her military campaigns turned the tide of the war — most importantly, she broke the Siege of Orléans in 1429, an event that changed the course of French history. She was subsequently captured by Burgundian forces, sold to the English, tried for heresy, and burned at the stake in Rouen at approximately age 19.

Joan of Arc was declared a martyr and ultimately canonized as a saint by the Catholic Church in 1920 — nearly 500 years after her death. She has become one of the most iconic figures in Western history, inspiring centuries of art, literature, and political movements. She is simultaneously a Catholic saint, a French national heroine, a symbol of feminine courage, and a figure appropriated by diverse political ideologies ranging from French nationalism to feminism. Her image — armored, on horseback, carrying a standard — is immediately recognizable worldwide.

Portland's Joan of Arc statue reflects the city's connection to French culture through immigration and cultural exchange, as well as the strong Catholic presence in early Oregon history. The French name "Portland" — chosen in 1845 by a coin toss between two founders from Massachusetts and Maine — is coincidental, but the city has maintained French cultural connections. The equestrian statue of Joan of Arc in Portland joins a tradition of such monuments in cities across the United States and France, honoring a figure who transcends her medieval origins to speak to enduring themes of courage, faith, and resistance.

Nearby Attractions

Portland Art Museum

The oldest art museum on the West Coast and one of the top 25 art museums in the United States, the Portland Art Museum houses a permanent collection spanning 35,000 works. The Native American collection is particularly notable. The museum is located in the South Park Blocks, a linear park cutting through downtown Portland that also hosts multiple cultural institutions.

Oregon Historical Society Museum

Also located in the South Park Blocks, the Oregon Historical Society houses a comprehensive collection of Oregon historical documents, photographs, and artifacts. The museum's trompe l'oeil murals on its exterior walls are Portland landmarks in themselves. Rotating exhibitions explore Oregon history from Indigenous cultures to contemporary events.

Lan Su Chinese Garden

A classical Suzhou-style garden in Portland's Chinatown district, Lan Su was built by artisans from Suzhou, China — Portland's sister city. The walled garden features traditional architecture, koi ponds, teahouse, and seasonal plantings that create a tranquil urban retreat. It represents Portland's diverse cultural connections and its significant Chinese immigrant history.
