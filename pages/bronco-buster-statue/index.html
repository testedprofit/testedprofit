About the "Bronco Buster"

Located prominently in Denver's Civic Center Park, the "Bronco Buster" is a bronze sculpture that embodies the raw energy and skill associated with the American cowboy and the frontier spirit. Dedicated in 1920, this iconic work is a significant piece of Denver's public art collection. It captures a dynamic moment of a cowboy wrestling to stay atop a bucking horse, symbolizing themes of control, strength, and the challenging nature of the untamed West.

The Cowboy and the Wild Horse

The "Bronco Buster" depicts a rugged cowboy fighting to maintain his seat on a rearing, plunging horse. Details like the cowboy's quirt (a short riding whip) in one hand and his grip on the mane and reins in the other convey the intensity of the struggle. The sculpture masterfully captures the vigorous movement and the lifelike quality of both horse and rider. The horse's neck twists, counterbalancing the cowboy's sharp pull, creating a moment of dramatic, suspended energy.

The model for the cowboy in this sculpture was reportedly Bill "Slim" Ridings. Notably, Ridings was arrested for cattle rustling during the modeling process, and the artist, Alexander Phimister Proctor, reportedly paid his bail to allow the completion of the statue.

Behind the Bronze: The Casting Process

Creating a monumental bronze sculpture like the "Bronco Buster" was a complex endeavor, typically involving several stages and a close collaboration between the artist and a specialized foundry.

Initial Modeling:

Proctor would have first created a detailed model in clay or wax, capturing the intricate pose and dynamic energy. This initial model allowed for refinement and adjustment of the composition.

Plaster Mold Creation:

From the clay or wax model, a plaster mold would be made. This plaster model served as the master for the bronze casting process.

Bronze Casting (Lost-Wax Method):

While earlier American bronzes often used the sand-casting method, by the early 20th century, the lost-wax (cire perdu) method had become the preferred technique for fine art sculpture due to its ability to capture finer details and allow for more complex compositions in a single piece. In this process, a flexible gelatin or glue mold is made from the plaster. A wax positive is then created from this flexible mold. This wax model is encased in an investment material (a ceramic-like shell), heated to melt out the wax (hence "lost wax"), leaving a hollow cavity. Molten bronze is then poured into this cavity. Once cooled, the outer mold is broken away to reveal the raw bronze sculpture.

Finishing and Patination:

After casting, the bronze sculpture undergoes extensive finishing, including chasing (refining surface details), welding seams if multiple sections were cast, and applying a patina. The patina, achieved through chemical treatments and heat, gives the bronze its characteristic color and protective layer, contributing significantly to the artwork's final appearance and longevity.

The Artist: Alexander Phimister Proctor

Master of Western Sculpture

Denver's "Bronco Buster" was created by **Alexander Phimister Proctor** (1860-1950), a distinguished American sculptor often referred to as one of the nation's foremost "animaliers" (sculptors specializing in animals). Proctor's early life in the frontier town of Denver, starting in 1871, deeply influenced his artistic focus on Western subjects, including wildlife and figures from cowboy and Native American life.

Proctor's artistic journey included studies at the National Academy of Design and The Art Students League in New York City, and further training in Paris at the Acad√©mie Julian and Acad√©mie Colarossi. His reputation grew with commissions for the 1893 World's Columbian Exhibition in Chicago, where he displayed numerous Western animals and large equestrian monuments of Native Americans. He was known for blending the classical Beaux-Arts style with an authentic American vigor and dignity, emphasizing realism and dynamic action in his works. His deep personal connection to the West, which included living with the Crow Indians and modeling at events like the Pendleton Round-up Rodeo in Oregon, allowed him to imbue his sculptures with remarkable authenticity and kinetic energy.

Historical Context and Enduring Legacy

The "Bronco Buster" was commissioned as part of a larger vision for Denver's Civic Center Park, heavily influenced by the **"City Beautiful" movement** of the early 20th century. This movement aimed to enhance cities through grand, neoclassical architecture, urban planning, and monumental public art, often reflecting civic ideals and regional identity.

Commissioned by Mayor Robert Speer's administration and presented to the city by John Kernan Mullen in 1920, the "Bronco Buster" is paired with another significant statue in Civic Center Park, "On the War Trail," also by Proctor. Together, these two sculptures were intended to provide a visual narrative of Colorado's Western origins and the broader frontier era, celebrating the pioneering spirit and challenging life on the plains.

While these statues celebrate the spirit of pioneering and the challenging life of the cowboy, it is also important to acknowledge that they represent one particular perspective of Western expansion. Public art from this era often highlighted themes of "taming" nature and land, which, from a contemporary viewpoint, may overlook or simplify the complex histories of Indigenous peoples and the diverse narratives of the West. The "Bronco Buster" remains a significant historical and artistic landmark in Denver, inviting ongoing reflection on the many layers of Western American heritage and its evolving interpretation.
