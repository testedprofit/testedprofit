The Triad

A massive 26-foot-tall monument featuring three figures representing the region's early colonial history. Bienville stands in the center, flanked by a seated Native American to his right and a French priest (Father Athanase Douay) to his left.

Artist: Angela Gregory (Louisiana sculptor). Cast in Paris, France.

Relocated Legacy

Originally commissioned in 1951 and inaugurated in 1955 outside the Union Passenger Terminal. It was relocated to its current home in the French Quarter in November 1996, placing the founder closer to the historic grid he established.

History & Background

Jean-Baptiste Le Moyne de Bienville was born in Montreal, New France in 1680, one of eleven brothers who shaped the early history of French North America. He served as the colonial governor of Louisiana on four separate occasions, spanning the period from 1701 to 1743 — a tenure that gave him extraordinary influence over the development of the Gulf South. He is universally recognized as the founder of New Orleans.

In 1718, Bienville selected a crescent of relatively high ground on the east bank of the Mississippi River, about 100 miles from the Gulf, as the site for a new capital city. The location was chosen for its strategic position controlling traffic on the river, access to Lake Pontchartrain, and proximity to indigenous trade networks. The settlement was named La Nouvelle-Orléans in honor of the regent Philippe II, Duke of Orléans. Bienville understood the site's vulnerability to flooding — the land was barely above sea level and prone to inundation from both the river and the lake — but proceeded with settlement nonetheless, establishing a pattern of risk and resilience that has defined New Orleans ever since.

Bienville governed during extraordinarily difficult years, managing conflicts with the Natchez and Chickasaw nations, navigating the complex politics of French colonial administration, and dealing with repeated flooding and disease that threatened the settlement's survival. He returned to France in 1743 and died in Paris in 1767, never returning to the city he founded. The statue in his honor stands in New Orleans as a recognition of the man whose persistence and vision — despite the formidable odds against a settlement in the Mississippi Delta — produced one of the world's great cities.

Nearby Attractions

Jackson Square & The Cabildo

The heart of the original French colonial settlement, Jackson Square (originally Place d'Armes) was the ceremonial center of New Orleans from its founding. The Cabildo — the colonial government building — now houses exhibits tracing New Orleans from French colonization through the Louisiana Purchase, providing essential context for understanding Bienville's era and legacy.

Historic New Orleans Collection

At 533 Royal Street in the French Quarter, this research center and museum houses one of the finest collections of maps, documents, and photographs related to New Orleans and Louisiana history. Its changing exhibitions regularly feature material from the French colonial period, including maps and documents from Bienville's era. The Williams Research Center at 410 Chartres Street houses the full archival collection.

St. Louis Cemetery No. 1

The oldest surviving cemetery in New Orleans (established 1789), St. Louis Cemetery No. 1 contains the above-ground tombs characteristic of New Orleans burial practice — a response to the high water table that makes below-ground burial impractical. Guided tours are required for entry and provide stories of the notable figures buried here, including Marie Laveau, the 19th-century Voodoo queen.
